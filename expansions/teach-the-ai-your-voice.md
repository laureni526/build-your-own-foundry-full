# Teach the AI Your Voice — Your Language Page as Standing Instructions

*Expansion pack · self-paced · requires a Stage 4 Language Page ·
companion to `ai-thinking-partner.md`*

> Your Language Page is already a spec. This pack turns it into
> standing instructions — so the AI starts from your voice instead of
> drifting toward everyone's.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

# When You're Ready (the gate)

This pack has one prerequisite, and it's a real one: a **Language
Page** with phrases that have *recurred* — words you've caught
yourself using across different pieces of work, contrasts you keep
reaching for, things you'd never say. That's a Stage 4 artifact.

If your Language Page is thin or aspirational — phrases you'd *like*
to be known for rather than phrases that keep showing up — stop here.
An AI trained on an aspirational voice produces someone you're
impersonating. An AI trained on an earned voice produces you on a
good day. The difference is the same evidence rule as everything
else: not yet, keep observing.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

# The Problem, Honestly Stated

Every AI assistant has a house voice — competent, smooth, and
identical for everyone. Ask it to "write this in my style" in a fresh
conversation and you'll get the house voice wearing one of your hats.

The guardrails in `ai-thinking-partner.md` already gave you the
defensive rule: *when the AI's version sounds better but doesn't
sound like you, keep yours.* This pack is the offensive version of
the same rule: give the AI standing instructions built from your
Language Page, so "sounds like you" is the starting point, not a
correction you make on every draft.

The principle underneath is one your Foundry already runs on:
**rules in files, not requests in prompts.** (Its production twin,
`practice-documents.md`, applies the same idea to whole
deliverables.)

------------------------------------------------------------------------

# The Voice File — What Goes In It

A voice file is one page. Six sections, every one of them traceable
to your Language Page or your stories — nothing invented for the
occasion:

1.  **The one rule underneath everything.** The single test any
    sentence in your voice must pass. Yours will differ from anyone
    else's; that's the point. If your Foundry has a North Star
    question, it usually lives here in disguise.

2.  **Preferred vocabulary.** The 8–12 words that carry your thinking
    in miniature. Taken straight from the Language Page — the words
    that *recur*, not the words that impress.

3.  **Signature phrases — with a dosage warning.** Your earned,
    memorable expressions. Then write this instruction next to them:
    *use one or two per piece, never stacked.* An AI given ten
    signature phrases will use all ten in every paragraph, and your
    voice becomes a parody of itself.

4.  **Favorite contrasts.** If you argue by contrast ("don't do X, do
    Y"), list the real ones you use. These transfer to an AI better
    than any adjective — "warm but direct" means nothing; "don't
    change the person, change the conditions" is executable.

5.  **What this voice never does.** The anti-pattern list, again —
    it's the highest-leverage section here too. "Never opens with a
    framework." "Never gives ten recommendations when one will do."
    "Never uses exclamation points to manufacture enthusiasm." Name
    the exact failures; vague rules produce vague compliance.

6.  **The internal test.** Two or three questions to run before any
    draft ships. These are for you *and* the AI — put them in the
    file and the AI applies them; keep them in your head and only you
    do.

# Installing It (any tool — capability travels)

The file is plain text. Where it lives depends on your tool, and the
tool genuinely doesn't matter:

-   **Claude Code / agent tools:** save it as a skill with a
    description that says *when* to trigger it ("whenever drafting
    content that carries my name").
-   **Claude Projects / ChatGPT custom instructions / Gems:** paste
    it into the project or custom-instructions field once; it applies
    to every conversation there.
-   **Anything else, now or in five years:** paste it at the top of
    the conversation. Standing instructions by hand — one extra step,
    same result.

**Layering, once the core voice works:** if you write for genuinely
different audiences (your professional community vs. clients vs. your
own philosophy work), resist writing three complete voices. Write
*one* core voice file, then a short calibration layer per audience —
what shifts (formality, vocabulary, what you assume the reader knows)
and what never shifts. The core stays single-source; the layers stay
small. This mirrors how your whole Foundry versions things: one
canon, contextual expressions.

# The Guardrails (extended from `ai-thinking-partner.md`)

1.  **The file describes; it never replaces.** The AI drafts in your
    voice; you decide what's true, what ships, and what sounds like
    you on the day. A voice file is a starting point, not an
    autopilot.

2.  **Drift check, monthly-ish.** Reread something the AI drafted a
    few weeks ago. If you wince — if it's using your phrases in ways
    you wouldn't — the file needs a sharper "never does" section, not
    more signature phrases.

3.  **The evolution rule (this one is load-bearing):** a phrase gets
    *added* to the voice file only when it has recurred naturally
    across multiple pieces of real work — the same promotion
    discipline as everything else in your Foundry. Never add a phrase
    because the AI generated it and it sounded good. That's the
    generic creeping back in through the front door, wearing your
    name tag.

4.  **Your stories stay yours.** The voice file may *reference* your
    stories ("argues from named evidence, like the [X] story") but
    the AI still never writes new stories into your Foundry. Voice is
    delegatable; evidence is not.

# One Meaningful Next Step

Don't write the voice file today. Open your Language Page and mark
which phrases have actually recurred — three or more real uses,
different contexts. Those marks *are* sections 2 and 3 of your voice
file; the file becomes an evening's work once the marking is honest.

And if almost nothing is marked when you finish? You just learned
your Language Page was aspiration, not evidence — which is worth more
than the voice file would have been. Keep writing. The phrases that
are really yours will insist on coming back.
