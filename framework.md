*Build Your Own Foundry — Workshop Framework*

# The Problem This Solves

People with deep lived expertise — coaches, consultants, educators,
leaders, builders — carry a career's worth of hard-won knowledge in
their heads. When they try to make it reusable, they almost always
start the same way: they sit down to write their philosophy.

**"My 10 Leadership Principles." "My Coaching Framework." "What I
Believe About Change."**

And it almost always produces the same result: generic platitudes that
could have been written by anyone, that don't sound like them, and that
they never use again.

The failure isn't a lack of wisdom. It's a direction error. They're
building **top-down** — principles first, evidence never — when durable
personal systems are built **bottom-up**: stories first, patterns
noticed, principles *earned*.

Foundry itself is the proof. Its Repository History records that the
Constitution and Working Canon were written only *after* years of
stories revealed that a coherent philosophy was already emerging. The
foundational documents came last, not first.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

# The Core Mechanism (The One Exportable Idea)

Everything in this workshop rests on one discipline, taken directly
from Foundry's Promotion Criteria:

``` text
Observation → Story → Pattern → Practice → Principle
```

Each stage requires more evidence than the one before it. The default
answer to "should this become a principle?" is always:

> **Not yet. Keep observing.**

One story is an anecdote. Several stories from *different contexts*
suggest a pattern. A pattern that survives testing becomes a practice.
Only practices that keep proving true — across time, contexts, and
people — earn their way into your principles.

This is the entire system. Everything else is scaffolding around it.

# The Minimum Viable Foundry — Five Components

What a person can honestly start in two hours. Not Lauren's full
11-layer architecture — the smallest version that is complete enough
to grow.

## 1. One Question (your North Star)

Not a mission statement. A **question** — the one your work keeps
trying to answer.

Foundry's is: *"How do we create the conditions where people can do
their best thinking, their best learning, and their best work?"*

A question outperforms a mission statement for a system this young
because a question invites evidence; a statement demands defense. Your
question will be slightly wrong on day one. That's expected. It gets
sharper as your stories accumulate.

**Test:** every story you capture should relate to it somehow. If your
best stories keep escaping the question, the question is too small.

## 2. First Stories (3–5 story cards)

The seed evidence. Real moments from your work and life that changed
how you think — captured in a consistent, minimal structure:

-   **What happened** (the concrete event — names, stakes, specifics)
-   **What it revealed** (the transferable lesson)
-   **Where else you've seen it** (even a hunch)

Stories are not autobiography. They are evidence. The specificity is
the value — "a client once surprised me" teaches nothing; "our newest
analyst cut a 24-hour monthly process to five minutes and only told me
after she built it" teaches everything.

## 3. One Pattern Hunch

Look across your first stories and ask: *what keeps repeating?*

Write it down — explicitly labeled as a **hunch**, not a truth. The
label matters. It gives you permission to be wrong, and it protects the
system from your own enthusiasm. A hunch graduates to a named pattern
only when new stories, from different contexts, keep confirming it.

## 4. An Open Questions Page

The single most essential piece of governance — and the only one you
need on day one. A living page that holds:

-   Questions you can't answer yet
-   Hunches waiting for more evidence
-   Ideas you're tempted to promote but shouldn't yet

Foundry's own version says it best: *"Never resolve a question simply
because uncertainty feels uncomfortable."* This page is what prevents
your system from filling up with untested certainties. It is the home
for uncertainty — and uncertainty is a feature, not a flaw.

## 5. A Capture Habit

The system only compounds if it's fed. The minimum viable habit:

-   **One story a week** (10 minutes — use the story card structure)
-   **One pattern check a month** (30 minutes — read your stories and
    ask what's repeating; promote a hunch only if the evidence is
    there)

Small actions compound. A year of this discipline is 50 stories and 12
honest reviews — more documented evidence than most experts capture in
a career.

# The One Rule

If a personal Foundry keeps only one rule, it's this:

> **Nothing becomes a principle until multiple stories from different
> contexts support it.**

Different contexts is the load-bearing phrase. Three stories from the
same job prove something about that job. The same lesson appearing at
work, in your volunteering, and in your family life is telling you
something about how the world works — and that's what deserves to be
written as a principle.

# What Deliberately Waits

The full Foundry architecture is the destination, not the starting
point. Mapping the growth honestly:

| Stage | What emerges | What it corresponds to in Foundry |
|---|---|---|
| **Day 1** (this workshop) | One Question, 3–5 stories, one hunch, Open Questions page, capture habit | Story Library seed + Open Questions |
| **Months 1–3** | Stories accumulate; hunches become named patterns | Pattern Library |
| **Months 3–6** | First 2–3 principles earn their way in; recurring questions you ask others become a list | Working Canon (miniature) + Question Library |
| **Months 6–12** | Tested interventions ("when I see X, I do Y"); your recurring language starts to surface | Design System + Voice System |
| **Year 1+** | Beliefs stable enough to be foundational; enough history that you need to record *why* you decided things | Constitution + full Governance |

**Do not write your Constitution in this workshop.** Foundry's own
Constitution says it should evolve more slowly than any other document.
A constitution written in an afternoon is a wish list; one distilled
from a year of evidence is a foundation.

# Answers to the "Foundry as a Framework" Questions

This framework exists to answer the open questions Foundry has been
carrying about itself:

**What is the minimum viable Foundry?**
The five components above: one question, first stories, one hunch, an
Open Questions page, and a capture habit — governed by the one rule.

**Which governance documents are essential?**
On day one, exactly one: Open Questions. The promotion rule travels as
a single sentence, not a document. Everything else (decision logs,
history, audits, knowledge maps) becomes necessary only when the system
grows large enough to forget itself — roughly the point where you can
no longer remember why you decided something.

**What should be templated?**
The *shape*, never the *content*: the story card structure, the
promotion path, the Open Questions discipline, the capture rhythm, and
the one rule. These are content-independent and transferred directly
from Foundry.

**What should always remain personal?**
The question, the stories, the patterns, the language. A Foundry whose
content is borrowed is someone else's Foundry with your name on it. The
worked example in this workshop uses Lauren's material to show the
*mechanism* — participants should leave with none of Lauren's content
and all of her method.

**Can other people build their own Foundries?**
Provisionally yes — the mechanism is content-independent, and it has
already worked once on foreign material (the Women Who Rule curation
applied Foundry's promotion discipline to a body of work built for a
different purpose, and it held). But per Foundry's own standard, this
question stays open until the workshop has been taught and
participants' Foundries have survived contact with reality. **This
workshop is the validation experiment.**

# Tools Note (Deliberately Last)

Lauren's Foundry lives in a GitHub repository. Yours doesn't have to.
A notebook, a document folder, a notes app — anything that lets you
write, keep, and reread works. The system is the discipline, not the
software. Participants should choose the tool they'll actually open
every week, and no tool question should be allowed to delay the first
story.
