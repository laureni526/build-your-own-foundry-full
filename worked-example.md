# Part 1 — Five Contexts, One Principle

## The stories came first

Over roughly two decades, in settings that had nothing to do with each
other, Lauren kept capturing moments like these:

**A Girl Scout troop.** Five-year-olds regularly led songs and
activities for teenagers. The older girls genuinely participated — not
because anyone made them, but because the troop's culture celebrated
contribution rather than hierarchy. *(Story Library, Story 7)*

**The same troop, zoomed out.** It had started as five girls. It grew
to roughly sixty-five girls and eighteen adult leaders — not through
recruiting drives, but through a culture where belonging and shared
leadership were designed on purpose. *(Story 6)*

**The PTA years.** Time outside the traditional workforce became
leadership development anyway — fundraising, event design, community
systems. Nobody granted a title. The environment expected
contribution, so contribution happened. *(Story 5)*

**A workplace team.** A team once described as only able to "answer
yes or no" on audit tickets ended up building its own AI tools —
including an automation one team member built entirely unprompted and
revealed only after it was finished. *(Story 14)*

**Two communities.** Women Defining AI and the CREATE Buildathon —
where women launched businesses and kept building months after the
events ended, because the environments treated contribution as
expected rather than exceptional. *(Stories 9 and 11)*

## Then — and only then — the sentence

Look at what those five contexts share. A scout troop, a PTA, a
corporate team, two professional communities. Different decades,
different stakes, different people. The same thing keeps happening:

> **People rise to the expectations embedded in the environment.**

That sentence is Working Canon Principle 2 — and in the Canon document
itself, its evidence list reads: *Girl Scouts. PTA. Zinnia. Women
Defining AI. CREATE Buildathon.* The principle is one line. The
evidence is five contexts and twenty years.

**The teaching point:** the sentence took a minute to write — because
by the time it was written, it was just describing what the evidence
already showed. Nobody sat down one afternoon and composed it. It was
*found*, in a pile of stories, by someone who had kept the pile.

That's the whole method. That's what your capture habit is building
toward.

# Part 2 — All the Way to Public Work (the extension)

For participants who ask "okay, but where does this *go*?" — here is
one idea traced through every stage of the promotion path, ending on a
conference stage.

**Observation.** Lauren notices, repeatedly, that people don't start —
not because they can't, but because they aren't sure they're allowed.
The environment rewards certainty over experimentation. *(This became
Pattern 2 in the Pattern Library: "Waiting for Permission.")*

**Story.** So she goes first — messily. She builds a Slack bot
connecting underwriting guidance to OpenAI. It technically works. Sales
finds it slower than the spreadsheet. Nobody adopts it. A public,
visible flop. *(Story 13: "The Slack Bot Flop")*

**Pattern confirmed.** The flop does something the org chart couldn't:
it gives the team visible permission to experiment and fail. A teammate
later builds a much stronger tool — inspired by the failed one. The
failed experiment became someone else's foundation.

**Practice.** "The leader models experimentation before asking others
to" hardens into a repeatable move, tied to existing Design Moves
(Design for Self-Permission, Design for Experimentation).

**Framework.** That practice becomes the first of five conditions in
the Self-Teaching Team Framework — *Go First, Messy* — alongside four
siblings, each traceable to a Design Move. *(Framework Library,
Framework 1)*

**Public work.** The framework anchors a 45-minute keynote for the
Humans + AI Summit — and in a striking validation, two different AI
models drafting the talk independently from the same source material
converged on nearly identical structure and naming. The system had
become coherent enough to reproduce itself.

**And the governance discipline held the whole way:** the framework
was only admitted to the Framework Library *because* it had been
validated in real work. The same rule applies to the very workshop
you're sitting in — the Minimum Viable Foundry model doesn't enter
Lauren's Framework Library until this session has actually been taught.

# How to Use This in the Room

-   **Part 1 is the teach** (0:08–0:22). Tell the five stories fast —
    a minute each — then reveal the principle and its evidence list.
    The reveal lands hardest if you show the actual Canon document
    with the five contexts listed under the principle.
-   **Part 2 is the answer** to "where does this go?" — use it in the
    close, or hold it for Q&A. Don't front-load it; on day one,
    participants need to believe in story cards, not keynotes.
-   **The Slack bot flop doubles as the facilitator's "go first,
    messy" story** in Exercise 1 (0:30). Using it twice is a feature:
    by the end of the session, participants have seen the same story
    function as vulnerability, as evidence, and as the seed of a
    framework — which is exactly the point.
