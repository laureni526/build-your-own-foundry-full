# Stage 3 — Test, Then Earn Your Principles

*The Solo Path · typically months 3–6 of the journey, but the gate
decides, not the calendar*

> You've been watching your patterns. Now you act on one, on purpose —
> and if it survives, you get to write the sentence.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

# You're Here If

You passed the Stage 2 gate: 2–3 named patterns, each with 3+ stories
from different contexts and an honest counter-evidence line.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

# What This Stage Is Really For

There's a difference between a pattern you've *noticed* and a pattern
you can *rely on*. This stage closes that gap in two moves:

1.  **Experiment** — deliberately act on a pattern and record what
    happens.
2.  **Earn the sentence** — when a pattern survives testing across
    time, contexts, and people, write it as a principle: one sentence
    that simply describes what the evidence already shows.

This is where most experts' frameworks and yours part ways. Theirs were
written. Yours will have been *found*.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

# The Work

## Run experiments (deliberately small)

Pick one pattern. Design one small, real test of it in your actual
work. Don't ask "will it work?" Ask "what will we learn?" — the framing
matters, because it makes a failed experiment a successful record.

Capture each one with the experiment record (`templates.md`): what you
tried, what you expected, what actually happened, what surprised you.
The surprises are the point — they're where your pattern gets sharper
or gets corrected.

Two or three honest experiments teach more than a year of passive
noticing.

## Write the principles that have been earned

When a pattern has survived — multiple stories, different contexts, at
least one deliberate test — write the principle. One sentence,
descriptive, in your own words. Under it, list the evidence: the
stories and experiments that back it.

Then stop at **two or three**. Fewer principles, more useful. A page
with three earned sentences and their evidence is a working canon; a
page with fifteen is a poster.

The test for whether a sentence is ready: it should feel almost
boring to write — because by the time it's earned, it's just
describing what your pile of stories already shows. If writing it
feels like invention, it isn't ready.

## Formalize your Question Library

That list of questions you keep asking others (started in Stage 2)
gets its own page now. For each: the question, when you reach for it,
and what it tends to unlock. These questions are as much a part of
your expertise as your principles — for some people, more.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

# Watch For

-   **Aspirational principles.** A principle describes what you've
    *seen*, not who you'd like to be. "Lead with empathy" is a wish.
    "People rise to the expectations embedded in the environment" is a
    description with a twenty-year evidence list. Write descriptions.
-   **Resolving open questions out of discomfort.** Some questions on
    your page have been sitting there for months and it's starting to
    itch. Never resolve a question just because uncertainty feels
    uncomfortable. That itch is the system working.
-   **Skipping the experiment.** It's tempting to promote a
    well-storied pattern straight to principle. The experiment is what
    separates "I've noticed this" from "I've tested this." Run it.
-   **Big experiments.** If your experiment needs a budget, a
    committee, or a quarter, it's a project, not an experiment. Shrink
    it until starting is easier than avoiding it.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

# How the Original Foundry Did This

Foundry's Working Canon Principle 2 — *people rise to the expectations
embedded in the environment* — is one line. Its evidence list reads:
a Girl Scout troop, the PTA, a workplace team, two professional
communities. Five contexts, twenty years. The sentence took a minute to
write, because by then it was just describing the pile.

And the experiments were real experiments, including public flops: a
Slack bot nobody adopted became the evidence for a pattern, then a
practice, then a framework taught from a conference stage. The flop is
in the library. That's the discipline.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

# The Gate — You've Earned Stage 4 When

-   [ ] You've run and recorded **at least two deliberate
    experiments** — outcomes in either direction count.
-   [ ] You have **2–3 principles**, each one sentence, each traceable
    through a named pattern to **3+ stories from different contexts**.
-   [ ] Your **Question Library** exists as its own page with at least
    a handful of entries.
-   [ ] At least one thing you once felt certain about has been
    **demoted or revised** by evidence. (If nothing has ever been
    demoted, the system isn't filtering — it's just agreeing with you.)

When all four are true, open `stage-4-from-principles-to-practice.md`.
